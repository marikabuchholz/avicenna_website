About the Journal

Given the Avicennian ethos of diligent and sincere pursuit of knowledge, we propose to critically examine Muslim societies from the inside, rather than the outside. For too long, these societies have been observed from the exterior as unexpressed objects, but need to be engaged with as knowing subjects. Although Islam is discussed, we focus on the actors, Muslims (be they religious or cultural), rather than the metaphysical set of ideas.
For an integral part of the Muslim world, the year 2011 (also the year Avicenna – The Stanford Journal on Muslim Affairs was initiated) began with the promise of change. Though we cannot ascertain the nature of these changes quite yet, they definitely mold the character of our project. We would like to express our best sentiments for the efforts of the people in the MENA (Middle East and North Africa). We cannot strive to speak on behalf of Muslim people without being a part of this people’s consciousness. There is a people’s desire to re-examine the Muslim identity in a modern world. This insists that our effort is not just informative; it is also introspective.
